01 · BUSINESS PROBLEM

Structure free-text impressions — and generalize honestly.

Thirteen synthetic diagnostic-lab clients store 679 imaging impressions (USG abdomen and chest X-ray) as free text — Bahasa Indonesia mixed with Latin and English organ terms, labeled normal/abnormal via rad_label (~60% normal). On this closed synthetic vocabulary every method looks near-perfect — so the project measures the honest question: generalization to a finding whose vocabulary is unseen, where a hand-written rule set and a trained model part ways.

03 · METHODOLOGY

Rules built around negation — then stress-tested for generalization.

01
Corpus analysis
Catalog phrasings, cross-language synonyms, and negation forms (tidak tampak, no, unremarkable).
02
Normalize + clause / negation rules
Split into clauses; flag abnormal only on an un-negated finding cue (“abnormal wins”).
03
Train real models
Genuinely train TF-IDF + LogReg and fine-tune IndoBERT on the same leakage-free split (seed 42).
04
Vocabulary-held-out generalization test
Hold effusion + stones out of BOTH the rule vocabulary and the models' training data; score all three on the now-unseen cues.

05 · INSIGHTS
Rules can't generalize to unseen vocabulary — learned models can
Hold effusion + stones out of everything and the rule system scores 0% recall where an unseen cue is the only signal — it cannot match a word it has never seen. TF-IDF recovers 58% from context; the fine-tuned IndoBERT reaches 93%. That contrast, not the 100%, is the story.
Negation is the biggest lever (in-distribution)
A naive “mentions normal” heuristic scored 63.8% and missed ~81% of abnormals — “cor membesar” sits beside the word “normal”. Clause-level negation is what lifts the rules to 100% on the closed vocabulary.
Synonyms bridge languages
One organ, many surface forms — gallbladder / vesica fellea / batu empedu; ginjal / kidney / renal — handled by a synonym map so the same rules span Bahasa, Latin, and English in one sentence.
Liver and lungs lead mentions
Hepar (325) and pulmo (314) are named most often — consistent with abdominal USG plus chest X-ray.

VALIDATION · HONESTY

Is the 100% too good to be true?

Yes — it is correct, but only on a closed vocabulary. Here is the honest generalization number, measured.

Reading note — the 100% is on a closed vocabulary
The data is synthetic from a fixed phrase set, so the held-out test shares its vocabulary with training. This is not classic overfitting (no train/test gap; the rules have no learned weights to memorize) — its only blind spot is an unseen finding term. To measure that blind spot fairly, two whole finding families — effusion + stones (10 cues) — are removed from both the rule vocabulary and the trained models' data, then all three are scored on the 274 impressions that use them (40 where the unseen cue is the only signal).

The rule system catches none of these — by construction it can only match cues it was written for. TF-IDF recovers 58% from surrounding context, and the fine-tuned IndoBERT 93%. Note that overall accuracy barely moves when one finding is unseen (such impressions are rare in the set) — which is exactly why accuracy hides this failure and recall on the unseen finding is the number to report.

06 · PRODUCTION PATH
Widen the held-out sweep
More finding families and an XLM-R comparison to map where the generalization gap opens.
Radiologist-labeled real reports
The only fully honest generalization figure needs a sample of real, human-labeled impressions.
Per-organ gold labels
Hand-label organ findings to score the structured extraction directly, not just the overall flag.
PACS integration & trends
Link findings to images; track organ findings per patient across visits.
